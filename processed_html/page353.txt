If you receive WPBN or WGTU over an antenna, you will need rescan your television channels starting on October 9, 2019. by Connor Hansen EMMET COUNTY, Mi (WPBN/WGTU) -- People in Emmet and Charlevoix County are concerned about a lawsuit filed by the Little Traverse Bay Bands of Odawa Indians. The tribe wants the state to recognize a treaty that gives it jurisdiction over a significant part of the Emmet and Charleviox shoreline. The land in question includes the city of Petoskey and Harbor Springs. " We ' ve always had the land in the state of Michigan, "  said  Regina Gasco-Bentley, the chair of the Little Traverse Bay Bands of Odawa Indians.  " This is where our ancestors were, but in 1855 some of our ancestors went to Washington D.C. and worked on the treaty with the federal government and this became our home lands. " Gasco-Bentley said the main reason they want tribal jurisdiction over the area is for child welfare cases. " It will help assist us better over our Indian child welfare cases, because right now that ' s kind of a grey area for the state, "  Gasco-Bentley said.  " Some of them recognize that this is our reservation and that we would have full jurisdiction over all of the child welfare cases. " With clearer jurisdiction, those cases involving tribe members would be dealt with in tribal court. " This would just clear up a lot for us and give us jurisdiction over our people and that ' s all we ' re asking for, "  said Gasco-Bentley. Some people are worried that they would have jurisdiction over a lot more. Local governments and citizens have entered the lawsuit as intervenors. " Tribal lands and tribal members would not be subject to local regulations, including zoning, "  said Gary Rentrop, the president of the Emmet County Lakeshore Association.  " You could hypothetically, in this situation, buy the property next door to me and put in a commercial establishment, maybe even a type-two gambling facility. " The Emmet County Lakeshore Association has gotten involved with the lawsuit, concerned mostly with zoning issues and taxes on businesses. " You ' re not going to be subject to property tax, so you ' ve got a one up on your competitor there, Rentrop said.  " You ' re not going to be subject to sales tax so you ' ve got a one up on your competitor there. " Regina Gasco-Bentley hopes they will be able to work through those issues. " The biggest fear that has been put into a lot of people, you know, that their property values could go down, the tribe could start taxing them within the reservation boundaries and the zoning, that ' s a big one, but we ' re saying hopefully we can work through all of that, "  Gasco-Bentley said. The trial is set to begin in 2018, but both sides said they are open to going into mediation before then.
