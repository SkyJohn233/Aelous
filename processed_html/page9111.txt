Here, we demonstrate a multipartite QSS protocol that is encoded into these quantum states with unitary operation pairs. This paper proposes a new method of constructing a QSS protocol for the symmetric  W  state. This scheme is more efficient than other existing QSS schemes because our proposed protocol uses only a single photon measurement and XOR to recover the secret KEY of Alice. Since Bennett and Brassard proposed the BB84 [ 1 ] protocol, considerable research effort has been focused on quantum communication and quantum cryptography. There are many interesting branches of quantum communication and quantum cryptography such as quantum key distribution (QKD) [ 1 ,  2 ,  3 ], quantum secure direction communication (QSDC) [ 4 ,  5 ,  6 ], quantum secret sharing (QSS) [ 7 ,  8 ,  9 ,  10 ,  11 ,  12 ,  13 ,  14 ,  15 ], and quantum teleportation [ 16 ,  17 ], among others. In the past decade, quantum cryptography has become a popular research topic in the field of information security. The combination of quantum mechanics with information theory has provided us new techniques for many tasks. QSS is one important branch of quantum cryptography, which combines quantum mechanics with classical cryptography [ 18 ]. Usually, in classical secret sharing protocols, the boss, say Alice, divides her secret message into two pieces and sends them to her two agents, Bob and Charlie, who are at separate remote places, for her business. Bob and Charlie can reconstruct the secret if and only if they collaborate. The advantage of secret sharing is that one of the two agents can keep the other one from doing any damage when they both participate in the process of doing business. Recently, this concept has been generalized to a quantum scenario. Quantum secret sharing (QSS) is likely to play a key role in protecting secret quantum information as well as in ordinary cases such as classical secret sharing. In the simplest form of secret sharing, Alice wants a secret to be shared with her two agents (i.e., Bob and Charlie) so that it can be recovered only when they collaborate. Using the same token, any one agent, say Bob, cannot obtain the secret information. That is to say, Bob and Charlie constitute an authorized agent group, each of whose single members, Bob or Charlie, is unauthorized. Recently, multipartite entanglement has been relatively less studied in terms of its information-theoretic aspects [ 19 ]. In general, in a multipartite QSS (MQSS) protocol, the boss splits her secret into many shadows (more than two) and then sends each shadow to each agent, but not all of them are honest. Just as in QSS, each agent alone cannot recover Alice’s secret. Only when enough agents collaborate (e.g.,  t  agents from  n  agents), can Alice’s secret be recovered, which ensures that no unauthorized agent group can elicit information about the secret. Many QSS protocols have been proposed that use different quantum states, including single photons [ 14 ,  20 ,  21 ,  22 ], Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen (EPR) pairs [ 8 ,  23 ,  24 ,  25 ,  26 ,  27 ,  28 ,  29 ,  30 ], and Greenberger–Horne–Zeilinger (GHZ) states [ 7 ,  31 ,  32 ]. A symmetric  W  state  \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(\vert001\rangle+\vert010\rangle +\vert100\rangle) \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(\vert001\rangle+\vert010\rangle +\vert100\rangle) , which is pair-wise entangled, is a different kind of entanglement state, that is, the symmetric  W  state cannot be transformed to a GHZ state under any local operation and classical communication (LOCC) [ 33 ]. These facts suggest that  W  states have physical properties that are considerably different from GHZ states, and that are rarely considered for quantum information protocols, whereas GHZ states have been used for quantum cryptography among several distant parties [ 34 ]. In 2004, Joo et al. [ 34 ] proposed a partial quantum secret sharing protocol (PQSS) that uses the symmetric  W  state of three qubits. Unfortunately, the PQSS is not a secure protocol because any agent can recover the boss’s secret without the help of the other agent. The challenge then, is how to use the symmetric  W  state to establish a secure QSS protocol. This paper attempts to meet this challenge. A multipartite QSS that employs the coding function proposed by Tsai and Hwang [ 35 ] is proposed, and through a security analysis of the proposed QSS protocol, it is shown that it is secure regardless of whether it is in the ideal quantum or the noisy quantum channel. This paper is organized as follows. First, Sect.  2  briefly reviews Tsai et al.’s coding function. Section  3  then describes the proposed QSS protocol and expands it to MQSS. A security analysis is also given here. Finally, Sect.  4  summarizes the contributions of this study and offers a conclusion. Suppose Alice wants to send a secret message to two distant parties, Bob and Charlie. One of them, Bob or Charlie, is not entirely trusted by Alice, and she knows that if the two guys coexist, the honest one will keep the dishonest one from doing any damages. The two receivers, Bob and Charlie, can infer the secret message only by their mutual assistance. Step 2 : Bob (Charlie) sends an acknowledgment to Alice upon receiving  T 1  ( T 2 ). Step 3 : Alice announces the positions and the corresponding bases of  T 1  ( T 2 ) to Bob (Charlie). Step 4 : Bob (Charlie) extracts  T C  from  T 1  ( T 2 ), and then they measure  T C  with these corresponding bases. Finally, Bob (Charlie) returns the measuring results to Alice. Step 5 : Alice checks the existence of eavesdropping based on the results of Bob (Charlie). If the detected error rate exceeds a predetermined threshold  τ  ( τ ≈2 %–8.9 % depending on the channel situation (e.g., attenuation etc.) [ 36 ,  37 ,  38 ,  39 ,  40 ]), she aborts this communication and restarts the protocol. In this section, we analyze four popular attacks against the proposed protocol: Intercept-and-Resend attack, Entangle-and-Measure attack, CE attack, and Trojan-Horse attack. The first two attacks were analyzed in by Tsai et al. as well [ 35 ]. Finally, we discuss the environment of the noisy and lossy quantum channel [ 44 ]. (i) Intercept-and-Resend: We assume that the quantum channel is ideal and that an attacker (called Eve) intercepts s photons sent by Alice, stores them, and resends a sequence of forged photons to one of the agents in hope to pass the eavesdropping check. Because of the decoy photons strategy, the probability of Eve’s detection in a public discussion is 1−(3/4) ( s /2) ≈1, if  s /2 is large. According the collaborative property of the QSS, Eve may not be able to obtain Alice’s secret KEY. For  n −1 agents, the probability of failure is 1−((3/4) ( s /2) ) n −1 ≈1, which implies that as the number of agents increases, the probability approaches closer to 1. (ii) Entangle-and-Measure Attack:In this type of attack, Eve entangles an ancillary photon on each of the traveling photons using a unitary operation  \widehat{U} \widehat{U}  (i.e.,  \widehat{U}^{\dagger}\widehat {U} = \widehat{U}\widehat{U}^{\dagger} = I \widehat{U}^{\dagger}\widehat {U} = \widehat{U}\widehat{U}^{\dagger} = I ), and then measures the ancillary photons to steal Alice’s secret KEY. However, similar to the analysis proposed in [ 41 ], if Eve wants to steal the secret KEY, the  \widehat{U} \widehat{U}  operation will disturb the decoy photons’ states. Therefore, Eve’s attack can be detected by performing an eavesdropping check between Alice and the agents [ 35 ]. (iii) Correlation-Elicitation Attack (CE attack): As described in [ 43 ], Eve first prepares an ancillary in the state |0〉 E  (say E). When Alice sends out one particle of the Bell state (say B), Eve intercepts it and executes a controlled-NOT (CNOT) operation on B (control qubit) and E (target qubit) to entangle her auxiliary particle. Thereafter, Eve sends B to Bob. Bob performs something for his needs (e.g., encoding) and returns to Alice. Similarly, Eve intercepts B again and performs another CNOT operation to disentangle them; then Eve measures particles E on {|0〉,|1〉} basis and sends B to Alice. It can be seen easily that her measurement outcome is absolutely correlative with the secret bit. Eve can definitely obtain the secret according to her result. In our scheme Alice sends encoded particles (shadows mixed with decoy photons) to each agent and all agents send nothing to Alice except their decoy photon measurements. Therefore, without the positions and states of the decoy photons, Eve’s CE attack stands invalidated. (iv) Trojan Horse Attack: Because our proposed protocol involves one-way communication, the eavesdropper has no chance to develop a Trojan horse attack. Case 1 : Eve may hide her attack by using the noise in the quantum channel [ 42 ]. The quantum bit error rate (QBER) caused by channel noise is  τ  which is about from 2 % to 8.9 %. If the QBER caused by Eve’s attack is smaller than  τ , a legitimate participant may not be able to detect Eve’s attacking behavior. However, in our proposed protocol, the QBER caused by such an attack is approximately 25 %, which is larger than  τ . Thus, such an attack will eventually be detected in the eavesdropper check [ 35 ,  44 ]. Case 2 : Eve intercepts the photons transmitted by Alice, holds some photons to herself, and sends the remaining photons through an ideal channel. Then, Eve can measure the intercepted photons on a  Z  basis to reveal a few bits of Alice’s secret. Fortunately, after slight modifications, our protocol is secure against this type of attack. In the concrete example (Sect.  3.3.1  (| W 3 〉) step 2 of our protocol), Bob and Charlie (agents) tell Alice (boss) about the received photons and the photons lost in transmission. Thereafter, Alice, Bob, and Charlie only use the lossless (received) photons, which will be discarded as unreliable owing to the eavesdropper be detected, to do public discussion and share the secret. Because the intercepted photons become meaningless photons, Eve cannot extract any part of information about Alice’s secret from these photons. Thus, our protocol remains security in the event of the above mentioned attack. This paper proposes a new method of constructing a QSS protocol for the symmetric  W  state. This method is based on an encoding function (unitary operation pair) that was proposed by Tsai et al. in [ 35 ], and we expand the unitary operations presented there to construct a QSS scheme. Almost all of the QSS protocols use the property of entanglement for the purpose of quantum sharing, and they always use entanglement measurement (e.g., EPR or GHZ) and some quantum computation to recover the secret KEY of the owner. The existing literature, however, contains no mention of a QSS scheme that applies in the symmetric  W  states. In this paper, we developed a QSS protocol from a tripartite | W 3 〉, quartet | W 4 〉,… , and expanded it to multipartite | W n 〉 using the property of these symmetric  W  states. These schemes are more efficient than the existing QSS schemes because the proposed protocol uses only a single photon measurement and XOR to recover the secret KEY of Alice.
