By Rattawut Lapcharoensap. 250 pp. Grove Press. $19.95. Here &# x27;s the problem with Write what you know: What too many aspiring writers know, it turns out, is that a suburban American adolescence causes vague feelings of sadness -- especially when one &# x27;s formative years include a dying grandparent or housepet. A way to avoid such tedium is to write what you don &# x27;t know, to labor toward peculiarity. The risk there is that your well-researched make-believe might come off as exactly that: a fake. It &# x27;s the lucky writer whose story is familiar to himself and exotic to his readers. For his debut, the skilled and very young Rattawut Lapcharoensap (he &# x27;s 25) has written seven stories that pique our interest because they &# x27;re set in Thailand, where he &# x27;s lived much of his life. When he &# x27;s really going strong, Lapcharoensap is a commanding, animated tour guide, and a lot more than that -- he can write with the bait and the hook of genuine talent. At his weakest, however, he leans on exotic atmosphere and little else. Like most of the pieces here, the opening story,  " Farangs, "  is told by an unnamed Thai youngster who lives with his mother, is wiser than his elders and is gravely conscious of Western tourists and culture -- perhaps too conscious. Lapcharoensap handles the discord between East and West ham-handedly.  " Need I remind you, boy, that the elephant is our national symbol? "  a character asks, just before an American girl -- described variously as  " angelic, " " some ethereal angel come from heaven to save me, "  and if that &# x27;s not unsubtle enough,  " my American angel "  -- stands triumphantly atop a  " decrepit elephant. "  Then our narrator watches, on television, Rambo wreak havoc on Southeast Asia. (We get it.) " Farangs "  starts with generalizations --  " the French like plump girls, "  the British come to Thailand to work on  " their penchant for hashish "  -- and the portrayals never get much more specific or nuanced than that. The Americans -- that  " angel, "  her boyfriend and the narrator &# x27;s absent G.I. father -- are the crudest of stereotypes. Even the narrator &# x27;s pig, named Clint Eastwood, is meant to be a symbol of the West &# x27;s cultural reach. (This has been done before, recently and funnier. In Jonathan Safran Foer &# x27;s novel  " Everything Is Illuminated, "  the Ukrainian boy &# x27;s pet is named Sammy Davis Jr. Jr.). What &# x27;s more, you can anticipate the story &# x27;s end from its first pages. Maybe not to the exact detail -- that the American boyfriend will try to tackle the pig, and that the narrator will throw mangoes at him from a safe distance. But you know that bigotry will end up thwarting the narrator &# x27;s desire for the tourist girl, and that some kind of ironic reversal will occur in the last paragraphs. It &# x27;s too bad this story opens the collection, because you &# x27;re tempted to give up on Lapcha-roensap after it. And, to be honest, even some of his better stories are occasionally laid low by imprecision -- of language, of detail. He can fall into shorthand that reads, in its paucity of descriptive effort, like stage direction:  " We walk silently through our neighborhood. The teashops. The dilapidated playground " ;  " She wanted to see what all the fuss was about. The fine sand. The turquoise water. "  Lapcharoensap reuses similes (too many things are said to be like maps or like flowers), and sometimes these similes fail to agree grammatically. Worse, he often -- very often -- gives his young narrators a sophistication that seems beyond them. One 11-year-old narrator says, about a prostitute:  " I wondered how many men she had held up tonight, how many more she would hold in the thousands of nights before her. I wondered whether she was already finding the force of their weight unbearable. " But whenever you &# x27;re about to throw up your hands, some fluent craftsmanship or inspired imagery lures you on. There are moments you can &# x27;t ignore -- a harrowing depiction of the young huffer whose face explodes into flame in  " At the Café Lovely "  or, in  " Sightseeing, "  naked, sagging breasts  " like upturned bells. "  Two superb stories work the whole way through:  " Cockfighter, "  an 89-page dazzler about a young girl whose father trains fighting cocks, and  " Priscilla the Cambodian. " " Cockfighter "  displays Lapcharoensap &# x27;s gift for the quick detail that catches not only his Thai milieu, but teenage life everywhere. And  " Priscilla, "  which describes gradations of poverty in the third world, is near-perfect in its lyricism, wistfulness and concision. Some recent debuts may be more consistent than  " Sightseeing "  is, but few attain its heights. Darin Strauss is the author of two novels,  " Chang and Eng "  and  " The Real McCoy.
